# Presumed Innocent: Central City Should Let the Court Do Its Job

**OPINION** — On September 16, the New York Attorney General unsealed a 79-count indictment against RCI Hospitality Holdings, five of its executives — among them chief executive Eric Langan — and three of the company's Manhattan clubs. The allegations are serious: prosecutors say the company bribed a state tax auditor to underpay roughly $8 million in sales taxes between 2010 and 2024. We do not minimize that. We also do not forget a principle older than any of us: in this country, an accusation is the beginning of a process, not the end of one.

An indictment is a prosecutor's argument that there is enough evidence to bring a case. It is not proof, and it is not a verdict. RCI has answered plainly, calling the charges "overreaching" and "baseless" and promising to defend itself. That denial deserves to be heard with the same seriousness as the charge.

Central City knows something about judging people too quickly. This is a town that built its modern identity on a complicated history and chose to meet it with humor and humanity rather than condemnation. We can extend the same basic fairness to a businessman who has not been convicted of anything.

None of this asks anyone to ignore the case. It asks the opposite: pay attention, read past the headline, and let the evidence be tested where it is supposed to be tested — in a courtroom, under cross-examination, before a jury. Until then, Eric Langan is what the law says he is: presumed innocent.
