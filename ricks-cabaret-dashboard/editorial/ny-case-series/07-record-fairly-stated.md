# The Record, Fairly Stated: What the Indictment Alleges — and What RCI Says Back

**NEWS ANALYSIS** — A defense-minded series owes its readers the prosecution's case, stated straight. Here it is, without spin in either direction.

**What the Attorney General alleges.** On September 16, New York Attorney General Letitia James unsealed a 79-count indictment against RCI Hospitality Holdings, five executives — Eric Langan (president and CEO), Bradley Chhay (CFO), Ahmed "Ed" Anakar (director of operations), Shaun Kevlin (a regional manager), and Timothy Winata (controller) — and three Manhattan clubs: Rick's Cabaret, Vivid Cabaret, and Hoops Cabaret. Prosecutors say the company bribed a state tax auditor with comped trips, hotels, meals and private dances in exchange for favorable treatment across at least six audits, allowing RCI to underpay roughly $8 million in sales tax on its "Dance Dollars" between 2010 and 2024, and that executives recorded bribe payments as "promotional" expenses. The top counts, criminal tax fraud in the first degree, carry up to 8⅓ to 25 years.

**What RCI says back.** The company "denies the allegations" and says it "will take all necessary action to defend themselves against these overreaching charges, while continuing to seek a just resolution." It calls the indictments "only allegations, which we believe are baseless," states that its policy is to pay "all legitimate and non-contested taxes," and notes that all three Manhattan clubs remain open.

**Where that leaves us.** Two accounts, sharply opposed, neither yet tested by evidence. The Attorney General must prove the charges; RCI is presumed innocent. Everything between now and a verdict — arraignment, discovery, motions, possibly trial — is the process by which those two stories get measured against the facts. We have laid out both so readers can follow that process with their eyes open.
