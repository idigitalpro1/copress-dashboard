# Due Process Over Mob Justice

**OPINION** — There are two ways a society can resolve an accusation. One is patient, procedural and often frustrating: charges, evidence, a defense, a neutral judge, a jury, a verdict that can be appealed. The other is fast, emotional and cheap: a headline, a hashtag, a verdict rendered in the comment section before a single witness is sworn.

The first is called due process. The second is called a mob, however well dressed.

We raise this because cases involving adult entertainment arrive pre-loaded with disapproval, and disapproval is the enemy of careful judgment. It is tempting to let the *kind* of business stand in for proof of the *crime*. But the law does not convict people for the industry they're in. It convicts them, if at all, on evidence — and it gives them a defense first.

RCI says the New York charges are baseless and intends to fight them. It is entitled to do exactly that, and to be judged on what the evidence shows rather than on what a press release implies or a reader assumes. That entitlement is not a courtesy extended to the popular. It is most important precisely when the accused is easy to dislike, because that is when the temptation to skip the process is strongest.

Let the court do the slow work. Mob justice is always available, always certain, and always — eventually — turned on someone we'd rather it spared.
