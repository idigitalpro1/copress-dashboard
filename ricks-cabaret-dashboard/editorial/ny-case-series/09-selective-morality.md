# Selective Morality and the Double Standard

**OPINION** — Tax cases are common. Bribery cases are common. What is not common is the cloud of moral theater that gathers around a case the moment the words "strip club" appear in the headline.

I want to separate two things that the coverage smashes together. One is the legal question: did anyone bribe a tax auditor? That is for a New York jury, and RCI says the answer is no — that the charges are baseless. The other is the cultural question: why does an adult-entertainment defendant get a tone, a vocabulary and a relish that an ordinary corporate tax defendant never gets?

You can hear it in the official language — "shamelessly," the gleeful airing of texts about "the girls." It is a tone designed to make you feel the verdict before you've weighed a fact. The same alleged conduct in a different industry would be a dry white-collar story on the business page. Put it in our industry and it becomes a morality play.

A town that celebrates Madam Lou Bunch ought to recognize that move and distrust it. We know better than most that the easy contempt aimed at this line of work is usually a substitute for thinking, not the product of it. Judge the conduct, if and when it is proven. Don't let the marquee do the judging for you.
