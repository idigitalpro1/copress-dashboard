# The Lou Bunch Mirror

**OPINION** — This Saturday, grown adults will push wheeled beds down Main Street while a crowd cheers, and we will toast the memory of Louisa "Lou" Bunch — Central City's last madam, who ran a brothel on Pine Street until 1914 and, when tuberculosis swept the camp, turned that brothel into a hospital. We don't whisper about her. We named a festival after her. For more than fifty years this town has chosen to look at its own bawdy, brawling history and *celebrate* it.

I think about that as I read the New York coverage of Rick's parent company. I want to be careful here, because a courtroom — not a column — decides guilt, and RCI says the charges are baseless. So set the specific allegations aside for a moment and consider only the posture: a culture that markets its red-light past as charming heritage on one weekend can turn remarkably grave about an adult-entertainment operator on another.

That is not an argument that anyone is innocent or guilty. It is an argument about consistency — about the comfortable distance between the madams we put on T-shirts and the operators we put on the front page. Lou Bunch was tolerated, then mythologized. We made her a hero precisely because she was more than the easy caricature of her trade.

Maybe the lesson of Bunch Day is not that anything goes. Maybe it is that people in this business have always been easy to condemn and harder to actually understand — and that the honest move, the *Central City* move, is to wait for the whole story before we decide which kind of figure we are looking at.

The beds will roll Saturday. The case will grind on for months. We can hold both, and we can hold our judgment too.
