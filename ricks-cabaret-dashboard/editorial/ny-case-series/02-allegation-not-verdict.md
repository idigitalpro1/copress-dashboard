# An Indictment Is an Allegation, Not a Verdict — Here's the Difference

**OPINION / EXPLAINER** — The number is doing a lot of work in the coverage: *79 counts.* It sounds overwhelming, and it is meant to. So it is worth slowing down to explain what a count actually is.

A criminal indictment is a charging document. A grand jury hears the prosecution's evidence — only the prosecution's — and decides whether there is probable cause to proceed. The defense does not present its case at that stage. That is why prosecutors so rarely fail to get an indictment, and why a long list of counts reflects how the government has chosen to slice its theory, not how much has been proven.

In the New York case, the Attorney General alleges that RCI executives bribed a tax auditor with comped trips and dances to settle audits for less than was owed, and that they logged the payments as "promotional" expenses. Those are the government's contentions. RCI says it "denies the allegations" and that its policy is to pay "all legitimate and non-contested taxes." All three of the company's Manhattan clubs, the company notes, remain open.

What happens next is the part that matters and the part that takes time: arraignment, the exchange of evidence in discovery, pretrial motions that can narrow or knock out charges, and — if there is no resolution — a trial where the government must prove each element of each count beyond a reasonable doubt.

We print the seriousness of the charges because they are real. We print the presumption of innocence because it is also real. A reader who walks away believing the case is *decided* has been misled — not by us, we hope, but by the gravitational pull of a big round number.
