# Proportion and Priorities

**OPINION** — Every prosecutor's office runs on finite resources and infinite possible cases. Which is why, alongside the question *did they do it?*, a fair public also gets to ask *was this the case worth this much firepower?*

The New York Attorney General has built a sprawling, document-heavy prosecution — 79 counts, a forensic-accounting backbone, years of investigation — around a sales-tax dispute and the alleged corruption of one auditor. The state is entitled to pursue tax cases; no one is owed a discount on what they legitimately owe. But proportion is a public value too. A tax disagreement that ordinarily lives in audits, settlements, penalties and interest has been elevated into a multi-defendant criminal spectacle with prison exposure measured in decades.

RCI's framing is that these are "overreaching charges" over contested taxes, and that the company pays "all legitimate and non-contested taxes." A reader is allowed to weigh that framing against the government's and notice that the gap between a civil tax fight and a 25-year felony is enormous — and that the choice to leap that gap is a *choice*, made by an office that announces these cases with a press release and a quotable line.

We are not the jury. We will not pronounce on the evidence. But we will say this: when the machinery of a state attorney general turns its full weight onto a single auditor and a single company's books, the rest of us are entitled to ask whether the punishment being sought is proportional to the wrong being alleged — and whether the same energy reaches the cases that never come with a camera-ready headline.
