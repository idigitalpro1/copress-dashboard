# Trial by Press Release

**OPINION** — Read the New York announcement again and notice its craft. It opens with a quotable line — "shamelessly used their strip clubs to bribe their way out of paying" — and salts the body with the spiciest texts the investigators recovered. It is written to be reprinted. And it was, almost verbatim, across the national press within hours.

This is how a modern indictment enters the world: not as a sober charging document filed in a clerk's office, but as a communications product, optimized for the headline and the clip. We understand the incentive. We also think readers deserve to see the mechanism for what it is.

A press release is the prosecution's opening statement delivered before anyone is sworn in — one side's strongest framing, with no cross-examination, no defense witnesses, and no judge to rule what's admissible. The selected quotes may be real; the *selection* is an act of persuasion. RCI's reply — that the charges are "overreaching" and "baseless" — gets a sentence at the bottom of the story, if it survives the edit at all.

None of this tells you whether the allegations are true. That is the point. The packaging is engineered to make you feel that it does. Our job, and our readers' right, is to resist the manufactured certainty and keep the burden where the law puts it: on the government, in a courtroom, under proof beyond a reasonable doubt.

Be skeptical of the spin. Be skeptical of *all* the spin — ours included. Then wait for the evidence.
